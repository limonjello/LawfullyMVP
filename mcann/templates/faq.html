Guide/FAQ

1
What are annotations?
For Lawful.ly, annotations are ideally short, plain language explanations of the key issues. This may be a term, a sentence, etc. We think that good annotations avoid the TL;DR phenomena, but linking to more detailed explanations on other sites is a great idea to allow users to find more in-depth explanations if they so desire.

The key for Lawful.ly is to provide the initial quick explanations so that the user can then determine for themselves which issues they either need to research more themselves or which they want to potentially take to an attorney.
2
Can I copy other information from the web?
You should not use any copyrighted information without the explicit permission of the author. Even if you do have such permission, you should link back to the source of the annoation, especially if there is more detailed information available on that site.

Play nice. When in doubt, cite back to the source. More info on this is covered in our Terms of Use.
3
What can you do if I find my copyrighted information on Lawful.ly?
Ideally, we would like to get your permission to keep the information on our site with a link back to your site. We want you to get the credit for your work, but we believe that Lawful.ly can generate traffic for you which increases your exposure. If you have us remove your data and the link back to your site, you lose that source of traffic.

We will absolutely honor requests to delete unapproved annotations which use the copyrighted work of others. Please just email us at admin@lawful.ly
4
Is anyone allowed to add an annotation?
Yes! We believe that the law belongs to everyone and you don't have to be an attorney to know the key issues of a particular issue. If you have lived through it then you may be able to explain it in a way that resonates more with other users. We certainly hope and expect that attorneys will be a key part of our community, and will be especially useful when it comes to evaluating answers, but we absolute want all members of the community to contribute.
5
Are you trying to get rid of lawyers?
Not at all. Rather, we want lawyers to be able to focus on delivering to their clients true value-added work. If a client can provide their attorney with a quality agreement that they generally understand, then the attorney can focus on the specific issues of the client. They don't have to spend their time (and the clients money) educating the client if the client comes in with a solid understanding of most of the key issues of a particular transaction or situation.

In the end, we want to see attorneys spend as much of their time being true counsel to their clients vs. scriveners or teachers.
